## Project Summary Description

Artificial Intelligence-generated images have reached a turning point in visual fidelity, making them virtually indistinguishable from authentic images to human observers. While these technological advancements are impressive, the maturation of Generative AI has fundamentally blurred the lines between reality and fiction, heightened susceptibility to misuse, and posed significant risks of social disruption. To address this issue, the research community has developed numerous detection models achieving high classification accuracy. However, most operate as "black boxes," providing a final decision without explaining the underlying reasoning. This lack of transparency creates a critical trust gap that hinders operational deployment in high-stakes domains like forensic investigation and journalistic verification.

To address the need for transparency, the recently proposed "Spot the Fake" (FakeVLM) framework introduced an innovative approach utilizing Large Multimodal Models (LMMs). Rather than functioning as a black box, FakeVLM leverages a standard vision encoder and a projector module to map visual tokens directly into the reasoning space of a Large Language Model (LLM). This architecture allows the system to move beyond simple binary classification; it can accurately classify an image as real or synthetic while simultaneously generating detailed, natural-language explanations of the specific visual artifacts that expose the forgery.

Building upon this foundation, the core objective of our project is to augment the FakeVLM framework. By overcoming the forensic blind spots of standard vision encoders, we aim to improve the model's deepfake detection accuracy, cross-model generalizability, and the precision of its artifact explainability.

The output of the generative model will be a binary classification decision (Real or Synthetic) coupled with a natural-language explanation detailing the specific visual artifacts or forensic traces that justify the decision.

## Proposed Methodology

The proposed methodology relies on a selection of standard synthetic image datasets, including FakeClue, and DD-VQA, to provide a comprehensive mix of authentic and manipulated images generated by various techniques such as GANs and Diffusion models. To address the limitations of current detection systems, we will study and augment the FakeVLM framework, which utilizes LMMs. Specifically, we will integrate Vicuna-v1.5 7B as the core language model component to generate textual reasoning based on injected visual tokens. 

To overcome the blind spots of native Vision Transformers (ViTs), we will develop a multi-domain feature extraction pipeline. This pipeline will incorporate spatial and structural extractors like Xception and Patch-based feature extraction, statistical methods such as Neighboring Pixel Relationship (NPR), physical analysis through Sensor Noise Pattern Analysis, spectral examination via Frequency-Domain Masking (FDM), and semantic alignment using CLIP-ViT(L-14). Through this approach, we expect to deliver a more robust, multi-dimensional detection system that bridges the trust gap by achieving higher accuracy across unseen generative models while providing precise, human-readable forensic explanations. 

Finally, we will evaluate the synthesis results using a dual-benchmark methodology. This comprises the ER-FF++ (Explainable Reasoning - FaceForecasting)  set to assess reasoning quality against human annotations for facial deepfakes, and the LOKI dataset to evaluate generalizability across diverse image categories. Classification performance will be measured using Accuracy and F1 Score, whereas explanation quality will be assessed via Consistency, Specificity, and Selectivity (CSS) alongside ROUGE-L. These results will be compared with the results of the base FakeVLM along with other commonly used solutions, like FWA, CDFA and SRM, also used as comparisons on evaluation on the original FakeVLM paper [1].

## Schedule

The project is structured into five sequential phases spanning nine weeks. 

Phase 1 (Week 1-2) focuses on the “Initial Proposal Delivery and Environment Setup”, culminating in the delivery of the proposal. During this period, the development environment will be established, the baseline framework deployed, and the necessary datasets acquired. 

Phase 2 (Weeks 2-4) is dedicated to the "Feature Extraction Pipeline", which includes the implementation of the spatial, structural, statistical, physical, spectral, and semantic extractors. 

Phase 3 (Weeks 4-6) involves "Integration and Initial Fine-Tuning", engineering the fusion mechanism to combine feature extractions into the LLM projector and performing early training for the Checkpoint Delivery.

Phase 4 (Weeks 6-8) encompasses "Full Training and Comprehensive Evaluation" across the ER-FF++set and LOKI benchmarks to calculate all designated metrics.

Phase 5 (Week 9) entails the "Final Review & Deliverable Preparation", where the group will analyze the performance delta and draft the final report for the Final Delivery.
